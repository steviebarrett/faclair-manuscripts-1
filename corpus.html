Manuscript: London, British Library, MS. Egerton 2899

London, British Library, MS Egerton 2899 [London, British Library, MS. Egerton 2899.] , consists of a 14th-century psalter and a 15th-century (?) martyrology owned by the Campbells of Glenorchy through the 16th to the 18th century. In around 1500, 'Aingil Dé dom dhín', a Middle Gaelic verse lorica invoking the seven archangels and the twelve apostles, was inscribed on the flyleaf. On the whole, beyond names of angels and apostles, it consists of praise of their virtues and allusions to the dangers against which they protect the poet. In terms of language, there are some interesting early specimens and also some distinctive scribal traits that merit further investigation as possible examples of Scottish dialectal influence.

Hands

[unk.] MacEwen (?) (Hand7)
Identification

A single hand (FnaG: Hand7) produces the entire text of 'Aingil De dom dhín'. This hand does not appear anywhere else in Egerton 2899 [London, British Library, MS. Egerton 2899.] and the manuscript contains no explicit clues as to the scribe's identity. Its cataloguers (British Library 2016 [British Library (2016), Explore Archives and Manuscripts, <http://searcharchives.bl.uk>, accessed 2018-05-30] : sub num. Egerton MS 2899; Flower 1926 [Flower (1926), Robin, Catalogue of Irish manuscripts in the British Museum: Vol. 2, London: British Museum] : 23) dates the hand palaeographically to c.1500.

Black (2011 [Black (2011), Ronald, Catalogue of Gaelic Manuscripts in the National Library of Scotland, <https://www.isos.dias.ie>, accessed 2018-05-30] : NLS Adv. MS 72.1.31) has speculated that he may have been a MacEwen, for two reasons:

  • The manuscript was in the possession of the Campbells of Glenorchy by 1523 at latest (see below); the MacEwens traditionally provided scholars to this family.
  • Certain unusual palaeographical features of this hand (see below) are shared with Neil MacEwen (fl. 1630).

Other Activities

The same scribe is also the sole hand in the first fragment (fols. 1–5) bound into the composite manuscript, Edinburgh, NLS Adv., MS 72.1.31 [Edinburgh, NLS Adv., MS. 72.1.31.] . This fragment preserves at least 13 poems in his hand about or attributed to Colum Cille, although its poor condition frustrates close examination of many of them. The genre as whole has been examined in detail by Herbert (2005 [Herbert (2005), Maíre, 'Becoming an Exile: Colum Cille in Middle-Irish Poetry', in Joseph F. Nagy (ed.) and L. E. Jones (ed.), Heroic Poets and Poetic Heroes in Celtic Tradition: A Festschrift for Patrick K. Ford, CSANA Yearbook 3-4, Dublin: Four Courts Press, 131-40] ). Four of these poems, as well as a text of 'Aingil De dom dhín', appear in Oxford, Bodleiain Library, MS. Laud Misc 615, which Herbert and O'Sullivan (1973 [Herbert (1973), Máire, and Anne O'Sullivan, 'The Provenance of Laud Misc. 615', Celtica 10, 174-92] : 175) date to 1532×1545 and associate with the circle of scholars working on Columban material for the Ó Néills of Tyrone, NW Ireland. Furthermore, the page layout of the first fragment in NLS Adv. 72.1.31 [Edinburgh, NLS Adv., MS. 72.1.31.] — oblong pages with long lines, often one quatrain per line — resembles that of Laud Misc. 615. Indeed, the quatrains of 'Aingil De dom dhín' in Egerton 2899 [London, British Library, MS. Egerton 2899.] are each also set out one to a line. This might suggest that the present scribe had intellectual contact with scholars in the north of Ireland and drew on similar literary resources.

Text Summary
Content

The poem is a lorica-style litany, invoking the protection of the seven archangels (one for each day of the week) and the twelve apostles, praising their merits, and alluding to the dangers from which through them the dovotee is preserved. Some manuscripts attribute the poem to Colum Cille (Flower 1926 [Flower (1926), Robin, Catalogue of Irish manuscripts in the British Museum: Vol. 2, London: British Museum] : 24) but these are all late witnesses. Such an ascription is absent from earlier manuscripts, including Egerton 2899 [London, British Library, MS. Egerton 2899.] , and there is nothing in the text itself to particularly support it. It does, however, have a tendency to be selected by early scribes interested in Columban material (.i.e. the present scribe and the compilers of Laud Misc. 615 [Oxford, Bodleian Library, MS. Laud Misc. 615.] )

Structure

The poem is bipartite. qq1–18 (in the Egerton 2899 [London, British Library, MS. Egerton 2899.] text) invoke the protection of the seven archangels, while qq.19–30 invoke the proetection of the twelve apostles.

O'Nolan (1912 [O'Nolan (1912), Thomas (ed.), 'Imchlód Aingel', in Osborn Bergin (ed.) and Carl Marstrander (ed.), Miscellany presented to Kuno Meyer, Halle: Max Niemeyer, 253-57] : 253) suggests that these might have originally been two separate poems, as the section on the archangels closes with a dúnad. This seems plausible, as neither section depends on the other for sense, and the final quatrain, bearing a unifying dúnad, does not relate specifically to the second section. However, a dúnad need not always indicate the end of a poem but can indicate merely a shift in topic. Also, as shown below, all manuscript witnesses preserve at least some of both sections. Thus, if they were originally separate, their conjunction probably happened prior to the text's archetype and nothing can be known of their independent textual history. Nonetheless, the possibility of distinct origins should be borne in mind when assessing the language.

Style

The metre is lethrannaigecht mór (51515151; abcb). The resulting short lines rarely come together within a quatrain to form a long developed sentence and more often contain a discrete prepositional phrase or short statement. The relentless interest throughout is the glorifying of God, the archangels, and the apostles, so the style remains distinctly panegyrical.

Social Context and Intended Readers

There is no glossing or annotation on 'Aingil Dé dom dhín' to indicate how readers interacted with the text. As it is placed on the flyleaf of a martyrology/psalter that was, or would shortly come to be, in the possession of an aristocratic family, one might speculate that the poem was used for devotional purposes. Just as the martyrology identifies feast days through the year and the psalter is drawn upon regularly in liturgical time, 'Aingil Dé dom dhín' identifies a heavenly protector for each day of each week. Whether the Campbells of Glenorchy and their associates would have struggled with the poem's occasional early forms should be considered, however: the poem could be more for ornamental value. On the other hand, the poem reseambles secular panegyric in terms of metre, imagery and tone. It has been pointed out (Flower 1926 [Flower (1926), Robin, Catalogue of Irish manuscripts in the British Museum: Vol. 2, London: British Museum] : 23) that Colin's father, Duncan, is attributed poetry in the Book of the Dean of Lismore (Edinburgh, NLS, Adv. MS 72.1.37 [Edinburgh, NLS Adv., MS. 72.1.37.] ), implying sustained interest in Gaelic literature on the part of this family.

The Egerton 2899 [] text aligns structurally with the Leabhar Breac [Dublin, Royal Irish Academy, MS. 23.P.16 (1230).] and Laud Misc. 615 [Oxford, Bodleian Library, MS. Laud Misc. 615.] texts. This is not particularly borne out by a comparison between the varia lectiones in O'Nolan's edition and the present witness, however. While the Egerton 2899 [London, British Library, MS. Egerton 2899.] text does not consistently align with the others against the Leabhar Breac [Dublin, Royal Irish Academy, MS. 23.P.16 (1230).] and Laud Misc. 615 [Oxford, Bodleian Library, MS. Laud Misc. 615.] , it often presents its own distinctive readings against them.

It seems fairly likely that the omission of O'Nolan's q7 was a mistake at a certain point in the tradition. That is, it is not a later addition. O'Nolan's qq5–7 (represented by qq. 7 (= 5) and 8 (= 7) in Egerton 2899 [London, British Library, MS. Egerton 2899.] ) list seven archangels, describing each of them as a “guarantor" (cuir). O'Nolan's q8 (= q7 in the Egerton 2899 [London, British Library, MS. Egerton 2899.] text) then calls them “the seven guarantors on God's behalf" ('Seacht cuir dar cend De'). Given that three of these guarantors (Raphael, Painiel, and Gabriel) are enumerated in O'Nolan's q7, it was almost certainly in the original composition. The Leabhar Breac [Dublin, Royal Irish Academy, MS. 23.P.16 (1230).] , Laud Misc. 615 [Oxford, Bodleian Library, MS. Laud Misc. 615.] , and Egerton 2899 [London, British Library, MS. Egerton 2899.] texts thus all have a common ancestor somewhere below the archetype where this omission occured. This archetype must have existed by the period 1408×1411, when the Leabhar Breac [Dublin, Royal Irish Academy, MS. 23.P.16 (1230).] was produced.

As all other manuscripts of 'Aingil Dé dom dhín' are of Irish provenance, it seems reasonable to conclude in the absence of any other evidence that the poem was composed in Ireland. A relatively early stratum in the language suggests it was composed in the Middle Gaelic period.


Language and Style

The survey that follows draws solely on the Egerton 2899 [London, British Library, MS. Egerton 2899.] text unless otherwise specified, as the present concern is with the language of this scribe and with this version of the poem as a Scottish literary artefact.



Orthography and Scribal Practices
Spelling System

  • Vowels
  • A mixture of "ea" and "e" is used (e.g. "deamhan" (q29c

    ar deamhan dom dhin

    ), "spregadh" (q29d

    dom spregadh dom sparr

    )), with "-ea-" predominating.
  • There are no instances of "io" for "i".
  • There is occasional heterodox treatment of dipthongs (e.g. "naeime" (q12d

    le naeime is le neart

    ), acc. (or dat.) of noíbe). Sometimes, they are even simplified to a single vowel ("badal" (q15d

    ar badal ar bith

    ), nom. sing. of báegul). Otherwise, the expected Middle Gaelic form of the dipthong is typically used (e.g. "naem" (q19c

    in cure naem nua

    ), nom. sing. of noíb; "taebh" (q28b

    crist cechtar damh taebh

    ), dat. sing. of taeb).
  • Unstressed final syllables occasionally show signs of the confusion and simplification of the vowel to /ə/ that characterised Middle Gaelic (e.g. "mo [sh]laintisi air" (q9c

    mo shlainti si air

    ), nom. sing. of sláinte). However, they tend to remain appropriate to the grammatical case and number (e.g. "gom cridhe" (q28c

    gom cridhe gom cliabh

    ), acc. sing. of críde.
  • A distinctive feature of this hand is the use of "y" for "i" (Black (2011 [Black (2011), Ronald, Catalogue of Gaelic Manuscripts in the National Library of Scotland, <https://www.isos.dias.ie>, accessed 2018-05-30] : NLS Adv. MS 72.1.31), particularly in non-Gaelic names. This occurs in the present text: "symon" (q20d

    lim is symon seang

    ).
  • On two occasions, ríg is used for nominative (or possibly vocative) rí (q28d

    rig nime na naem

    , q29a

    Rig nime na naem

    ), anticipating Scottish Gaelic rìogh.
  • Accents
  • Accents are used, but erratically (e.g. "Da apstal dég de" (q19a

    Da apstal dég de

    , recte "Dá apstal dég dé").
  • There is a specific editorial problem with "i". The scribe routinely differentiates "i" from other minims (even when "i" is not in a minim cluster) through the use of an upward stroke that is inditinguishable from his accent. It is thus not possible to tell when, if ever, the scribe intended to mark a long "i" as í.
  • Accents are also quite erratically placed, sometimes even appearing over neighbouring consonants.
  • Consonants
  • Voiced and unvoiced dental stops in final position are usually lenited (e.g. "gruadh" (q14a

    Gabriel geltar gruadh

    ); "bith" (q15d

    ar badal ar bith

    )), but sometimes not (e.g. "urcanead" (q18a

    Gabriel geltar gruadh

    )).
  • Unaspirated voiceless velar stops in final position are typically not lenited (e.g. "sluag" (q1d

    sluag nach blaisde biadh

    )), with only one exception ("dluigh" (q4c

    beirt dainghean nach dluigh

    )).
  • Aspirated voiceless velar stops (/k/) are frequently used in place of aspirated voiceless velar fricatives (/x/) at word end (e.g. "nac[h]" (q10d

    nach egain an tolc

    ); "gac[h] domnaic[h]" (q14c

    gach domnaich dom dhin

    ); "toseac[h]" (q25b

    toseach na fear find

    )). While not universal, this is occurs too regularly to be explicable by accidental omission of lenition marks.
  • Lenition of bilabial stop "b" in final position to a labio-dental fricative is typically shown (e.g. "cliabh" (q28c

    gom cridhe gom cliabh

    )).
  • Lenition of bilabial nasal "m" in final position to a labio-dental fricative is sometimes shown (e.g. "ad chiamh" (q18d

    ad chiamh aingeal de dom dhin

    )) but more often not (e.g. "niamh" (q6d

    ban niam de ara dhréich

    )).
  • Final resonant "-nn" (/N/) is sometimes rendered as Middle Gaelic hypercorrection "-nd". For example, "ceand" is used throughout for cenn (e.g. "ceand" (q7a

    Seacht cuir tar ceand de

    )); "-nd" is generally preferred in the text overall by 6:1.
  • A dot, or sometimes a very small tick, is occasionally placed over the "d" in final resonant "-nd", giving "-nḋ" (e.g. "sginḋ" (q30a

    Lureach de fam sging

    )); this appears to be indicating that it is part of a consonant cluster and should not be taken as lenition, even though it resembles a lenition mark visually.
  • Initial Mutations
  • Lenition is marked (via a small tick) quite sproradically (e.g. "mo chul" (q22b

    ar mo chul re cach

    ), "dom dhin" (q29c

    ar deamhan dom dhin

    ), "a bharr" (q5d

    scath fhial blaith a bharr

    ), "mo [sh]laintisi" (q9c

    mo shlainti si air

    ); but "mo comla" (q25c

    mo comla nach gann

    ), "os ma cind" (q25d

    dom comhḋa os ma cind

    )).
  • Eclipsis is marked by nasalising initial vowels (e.g. "Seacht nárćhaingil" (q2a

    Seacht nárćhaingil and

    )) and voiced stops (e.g. "ar urcanead ngur" (q18a

    ar urcanead ngur

    )) and via voicing voiceless stops (e.g. "parrthalon na b[p]eann" (q20b

    parrthalon na bpeann

    )).
  • On two occasions, lenition is marked where eclipsis would be expected ("na cheall" (q2c

    Seacht cloidmhe na ceall

    ), "of the churches"; "na fheart" (q10a

    Uriel fial na feart

    ), "of the virtues"). This may be intended as a catch-all marker of whatever the appropriate mutation might be.
  • Occasionally, eclipsis is not marked at all (e.g. "toseac[h] na fear find" (q25b

    toseach na fear find

    )).
  • There are several examples of pronunciation spellings, in which the initial mutation is simplified or disappears entirely (e.g. "na b[p]eann" (q20b

    parrthalon na bpeann

    ); "muire an [fh]irog fhind" (q20c

    mure an fhir og fhind

    ); "ni gloir [fh]and" (q5b

    a gloir ni gloir fhand

    ).



Scribal Practices

  • Letter Forms
  • Black (2011 [Black (2011), Ronald, Catalogue of Gaelic Manuscripts in the National Library of Scotland, <https://www.isos.dias.ie>, accessed 2018-05-30] : NLS Adv. MS 72.1.31) has commented that this scribe employs a number of distinctive forms, compared to the norms of Gaelic scribal pratice at the time. Some of these are instanced in this text: for example, capital "T" consisting an upward stroke with a rightward half-stroke at top and bottom (resembling "[") (e.g. "T́igid" (q24a

    T́igid find pol rum

    )) or "y" for "i" in names (e.g. "symon" (q20d

    lim is symon seang

    )).
  • Double insular "s" is used to stand for "S" at the beginning of quatrains (e.g. "Seacht" (q2a

    Seacht nárćhaingil and

    )).
  • When "C" begins a new quatrain, it is slightly enlarged and a dot is placed inside the crescent (e.g. "Cor" (q5a

    Cor dibh michel mor

    )).
  • All other letters have their own capital form, used when the letter begins a quatrain.
  • The scribe regularly adds thin upward stroke above "i". This is very useful for distinguishing the letter in a minim cluster, but the stroke is indistinguishable from the length mark used elsewhere. It is thus not clear when, if ever, the scribe means to indicate a long "i".
  • Abbreviations
  • All abbreviations used in the text can be described as mainstream within Gaelic manuscript culture.
  • Most stand for just one or two characters (e.g. n-stroke, m-stroke).
  • Generic suspensions, standing for more lengthy strings of characters, are employed very rarely and only in the context of personal names.
  • Word Division
  • Spacing between words is in general quite irregular.
  • There is a tendency to use spacing to section off set prepositional phrases (e.g. "osmachind" (fol.iiir2

    biadhSeacht nárćhaingil andnarathmainḋir liumSeachtcloimdhenachealldomcoimdhe osmachind

    )), noun phrases ("molaintisi" (i.e. "mo [sh]lainti-si") (fol.iiir9

    Raphael leam doroithos cind gacha cuirmolaintisi airsamhairt is do is dluig

    )), and verb complexes (e.g. "nacbi" (fol.iiir17

    arsaint umni ac neamarni nacbi bailarcras iarcolartoil rebáis ḃhan

    )) and suchlike.
  • The scribe is also capable of rendering a sizeable portion of a quatrain with no notieable spacing between words, phrases, or even metrical lines (e.g. the rest of fol.iiir2

    biadhSeacht nárćhaingil andnarathmainḋir liumSeachtcloimdhenachealldomcoimdhe osmachind
