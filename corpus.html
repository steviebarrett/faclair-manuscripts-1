Manuscript: Oxford Bodleian Library MS. Arch. Selden B.24

The sole manuscript of The Kingis Quare and The Quare of Jealous, Oxford, Bodleian Library MS. Arch. Selden B.24REF TEXT HERE, which was produced sometime during the reign of James IV (1488–1513; Boffey & Edwards 1997REF TEXT HERE: 22) and which is also an important early witness to the works of Geoffrey Chaucer (d. 1400) and John Lydate (c. 1370–1451), contains a fragment of a Gaelic dán díreach quatrain. This has been inscribed by Domhnall Gorm, presumably a MacDonald of Sleat, and may be celebrating his relationship with a member of the Lothian-based Sinclair family, who commissioned and owned the manuscript, by lamenting his parting from the. Its fragmentary condition inhibits confident interpretation, however.

Hands

Domhnall Gorm ?Mac Domhnaill (Hand21)

The hand that produced the quatrain is identifiable as Domhnall Gorm only by the signature. This was a popular name among the MacDonalds of Sleat during the 16th and early 17th centuries so it is presumed to be one of this clan's chiefs. Interest in poetry on the part of the clan's senior members is attested in the next century through their patronage of Cathal Mac Mhuirich (Black 1976–78REF TEXT HERE: 332–339 Domhnall Gorm Mór seems to be the leading candidate due to him being known to have visited Edinburgh on several occasions (MacDonald & MacDonald 1904REF TEXT HERE: 34–35). In the absence of more detailed evidence, any explanation as to how he came to interact with the Sinclairs and their manuscripts must remain speculative.

Domhnall Gorm's hand is neat, professional, and his command of the orthography, language, and poetics of the learned classes appears on the whole to be sound. The possibility that the quatrain and signature were inscribed for Domhnall Gorm should be borne in mind. Assuming it is Domhnall Gorm's own hand, this is an interesting specimen of aristocratic high-level literacy.

Other Activities

No other works in this hand, or attributed to Domhnall Gorm, are known.

Text Summary
Content

This single quatrain in rannaigecht beag is not only lacking in context but is also missing two syllables each from lines a and c. As a result, its interpretation is difficult. The best attempt is a text and translation by William MacKenzie, with the assistance of William Watson (MacKenzie 1939REF TEXT HERE: 137), who acknowledges that their reading is uncertain. Their translation is as follows:

… (from the tears) of the meeting,
now that the world has sped its leafage
… (bank) close to a pool,
(that?) we have found on the surface of the round world.
I (am) Donald Gorm

In this reading the quatrain is celebrating some sort of fellowship or friendship, now gone. Its passing is compared to the fall of leaves in autumn. The friendship is compared to, or exemplified by, a scene on the banks of a pool. This intimate image is contrasted with its context in the vastness of the world, which presumably now separates the friends. The quatrain is followed by the signature, "misi domhnall gorm", Domhnall Gorm being a popular name among the chiefs of the MacDonalds of Sleat. If the inscription was made to celebrate the friendship of a Domhnall Gorm of Sleat and Lord Sinclair, or a relative, then this is most apposite.

There are, however, two major problems with this translation. First, cioth can mean "shower", "abundance", or "struggle" but "tears" is an apparently otherwise unattested poetic extension. Secondly, "air buain" as the perfect form of búainid would be a striking vernacularism in a formal and still early poem; it should surely be taken as a preposition (either íar or ar) and a verbal noun. and the subject need not be "bioth". Furthermore, "na coinne" need not be analysed as art. + noun; "na" could also be i n- + poss. 3rd sing. fem., forming the phrase "against her/it", possibly referring to bith on the next line.

A resulting alternative translation, with most of the changes in the first couplet, would be:

...to struggle against it,
against the world, to reap its foliage;
...{on the} bank, close to a pool
that we found on the surface of the round world.
I am Domhnall Gorm.

Other intepretations are surely possible. For the purposes of the present text and discussion, Mackenzie/Watson's reading will be followed, except for the substitution in of a more context sensitive interpretation of "air buain".

A text was also attempted by J. T. T. Brown, with a translation from Rev. Robert Blair (Brown 1899REF TEXT HERE: 151). However, Brown's transcription is so inaccurate that this is best left unconsidered.

Style

The metre is rannaigecht beag (72727272). All metrical rules are observed, as far as is visible. The tone is elegaic and lyrical, although, as noted above, its stance is still open to interpretation.

Social Context and Intended Readers

The quatrain is thought to have been inscribed during the 16th century. The manuscript was produced sometime after 1489 and a note mentioning the date 1592 appears below the quatrain on fol. 231v. While the quatrain could be later than this note, it is more likely to be contemporary or earlier (Black, 2017REF TEXT HERE: §127). This is also the period when multiple individuals named Domhnall Gorm, or thereabouts, were chiefs of the MacDonalds of Sleat and when the manuscript was still owned by the Sinclair family. Domhnall Gorm Mór (d. 1616) was in Edinburgh, as a prisoner, in 1589 (MacDonald & MacDonald 1904REF TEXT HERE: 34–35). Brown also identifies him as the Domhnall Gorm in question (Brown 1899REF TEXT HERE: 146). It is perhaps too fanciful to imagine him borrowing the Sinclair's copy of The Kingis Quair, itself written in the persona of a high-status prisoner, and inscribing the present text in gratitude and as his own reflection. Suffice to say, given the provenance and ownership of the manuscript and the likely status of Domhnall Gorm, this poem should be understood as courtly communication between aristocrats. Thus it joins other pieces of evidence for Classical Gaelic literacy among the late medieval Scottish Gaelic nobility (see also Black 1978REF TEXT HERE; Gillies Autumn 1978REF TEXT HERE; Gillies Summer 1982REF TEXT HERE). Indeed, it even implies an interest in Gaelic literature among the nobility far outside the core Gaelic world of the time.

Studies and Editions

The only useable text and translation of the quatrain is that by MacKenzie (1939REF TEXT HERE: 137). There is also the superseded attempt by Brown (Brown 1899REF TEXT HERE: 151). The manuscript is available in facsimile (Boffey & Edwards: 1997).

Filiation of Text

There are no other known manuscript versions of this quatrain, nor any known context from which it might have come.


Language and Style

Given the shortness, ambiguity, and lacunose nature of the text, the linguistic observations that can be made about it are naturally limited.

Orthography and Scribal Practices

  • Orthography
  • Long "í" is consistently "io".
  • The preposition ar (sometimes for for) is consistently rendered "air"; this variant has existed throughout the history of Gaelic (eDIL 2013REF TEXT HERE: s.v. 1 ar).
  • Lenition is marked where it would be expected to occur via a punctus over the consonant.
  • There are no instances where the marking of eclipsis would be expected.
  • Length marks are not used at all.
  • "{b}ruaich" is used, whereas the internal rhyme (: "fuair") requires "{b}ruaigh" (q1cREF TEXT HERE).
  • The later, slenderised nominative singular form of lám, "laim" (q1cREF TEXT HERE), is used.
  • Scribal Practices
  • Letter forms are standard Gaelic minuscule; the hand has a tendency to elongate some strokes relative to other hands. For example, the topmost horizontal stroke of his "s" and "f" stretches some distance over the character to the right.
  • His "a" is composed of a particularly elaborate four-strokes, like a square balanced on one of its corners.
  • Serifs are well-pronounced.
  • Each couplet of the quatrain is written on a single manuscript line.
  • Each line of the couplet is separated by a punctus.
  • Word division is largely maintained, although prepositions and possessives are placed in closer proximity to their nouns.
  • Contractions and abbreviations are standard and minimal; there are no generic suspensions standing for lengthy strings of letters, for example.
  • Capitals, if used at all, are not clearly distinguished, even in the signature.

Language
Nominal System

  • Nouns are nominatives or genitives, with one possible occurence of a dative "{b}ruaich" (q1cREF TEXT HERE).
  • An accusative ought to occur (re linne (q1cREF TEXT HERE)) but, as it is at the end of a clause metrical line, there is no opportunity to observe the mutations it triggers. The form itself could equally be an accusative or a dative.
  • No adjectives occur in this fragment.

Verbal System

  • There is only one verb in the fragment: "fuair" (q1dREF TEXT HERE), from fo-gaib. Given the lack of an object in the following line, it appears to be be a relative, although neither an analytic ending nor a particle is used to indicate this. Alternatively, it could be a simple verb, with the object appearing in the previous line for metrical reasons.
  • There is also a verbal noun: "buain" (q1bREF TEXT HERE). Its usage could be interpreted a variety of ways. It seems likely that, in conjunction with the preceding preposition "air", is playing either a quasi-infinitival role ("with the intention of harvesting") or the role of a past participle ("after harvesting").

Pronouns and Pronominals

  • Only one pronoun appears in the fragment: the emphatic "sinne" (q1dREF TEXT HERE). This appears to be the subject (or possibly the object) of "fuair".

Syntax

  • Given the lack of visible main verbs, the quatrain appers to be either a long single sentence comprising a series of prepositional phrases and relative clauses or merely a series of detached phrases.
  • A number of genitival phrases also appear (e.g. "clar na cruinne" (q1dREF TEXT HERE)).
  • There is one possible accusative ("re linne" (q1cREF TEXT HERE)), although whether it is being treated as such cannot be determined.
  • No adverbials are used.

Lexis

  • The fragment is dominated by images of nature, with a distinctly pastoral feel.
  • The vocabulary used seems to be fairly standard and approachable to one conversant in Classical Gaelic poetry.
  • Without more context, it is difficult to tell whether the language meant to be literal or allusive.
  • There is one possible piece of technical vocabulary: "coinne" (q1aREF TEXT HERE). This has a range of more general meanings — "company", "meeting", "against" (with prep. i n-) — but also the specific legal sense of free billeting (often anglicised in an Irish context as coigny. There is no particular reason to suspect that it is being used in this sense in the present fragment but the ambiguous context leaves the possibility open.
  • There are no loanwords in the fragment.

Conclusions

  • Not enough of the quatrain has been preserved to allow definite conclusions as to the linguistic register but the well-observed formal bardic metre suggests that this is to be taken as Classical Gaelic poetry. Such a conclusion is also reached by MacKenzie/Watson (MacKenzie 1939REF TEXT HERE: 137).
  • One possible instance of Scottish dialectal influence is "{b}ruaich" for "{b}ruaigh" (q1cREF TEXT HERE), against the need for a rhyme with "fuair" (q1dREF TEXT HERE), possibly showing Scottish devoicing of /ɣ'/ to /χ'/.
  • The verbal form, "fuair sine" (q1dREF TEXT HERE), if it is to be taken as a relative, may also show vernacular influence, since it lacks both a particle and a verb ending. However, neither is it Scottish Gaelic a fhuair and, given the lack of context, other interpretations are possible.

Style

As noted, the context of the fragment, both in terms of the rest of the quatrain and more widely, is lacking, meaning its use of language is difficult to interpret. It appears to be lyrical and elegaic, employing several images of nature or of pastoral scenes, possibly to describe the transience of things. This is all presented in a formal bardic metre and Classical language. It appears to be in the 1st person plural ("sine" (q1dREF TEXT HERE)), suggesting some sort of authorial presence, reinforced by the following signature. This implies that it is meant to be some sort of direct communication, although, from what survives, it appears to communicate largely by synechdochic allusion. The vocabulary is relatively mainstream, however, and there are no overt literary references to legendary people or places — although something may have been crammed into the lost four syllables — making the quatrain relatively approachable to anyone aware of the context but not necessarily highly learned in Gaelic tradition. All this reinforces the theory that this is a courtly communication between aristocrats, most likely a Domhnall Gorm of Sleat and a member of the Sinclair family.


Physical Description of Manuscrpt

Writing Surface

Paper; 27 × 18.42cm (Hunt 1895REF TEXT HERE: vol. 2:1, 614)

Foliation

Condition

Fol. 231v is badly eroded in places, leading to the loss of the first two syllables of lines a and c in the quatrain under discussion, as well as some text immediately above that may have offered context. The facsimile by Boffey et al. (1997) includes photographs taken under UV light, which unfortunately do not offer much improvement. However, the parts of the quatrain that have escaped erosion are very clear and present no problems.

Layout of Page

The quatrain is one of a number of notes, including several Scots verses, written in a single broad column in the centre of fol. 231v. The quatrain itself is set out one couplet to a manuscript line.

History of the Manuscript

Summary

Origin

A note in the main body of Arch. Seld. B.24REF TEXT HERE refers to James IV as king, meaning production of the manuscript must have been begun sometime after 1488 (Boffey et al. 1997REF TEXT HERE: 4). The appearance of the date 1592 on fol. 231v, among the spare leaves towards the end of the manuscript, imply that it was complete by this date, although this is uncertain.

Subsequent History

It is thought that it was produced for the Sinclair family in either Edinburgh or Roslin (Boffey et al. 1997REF TEXT HERE: 11).

Acquisition

The manuscript was bequethed to the Bodleian Library as part of the collection of John Selden (1584–1654) (Brown 1899REF TEXT HERE: 93). How it came to be in his collection is not known but the window between his collecting activities and the window in which the manuscript was composed is sufficiently narrow for there not to have been a significant intervening history.

Notes

1. The visibility of text drops to zero to the immediate left of the "a-" of "air", means that this might be the ending of a longer word (three syllables maximum) rather than the preposition air. In fact, lines ab, as now visible, are missing one of the two required perfect internal rhymes, and a word ending in "-uair" (: "buain") would supply such a rhyme. Nothing more can be said about this hypothesised word, unfortunately.

2. Some pen strokes are visible just above this line, before the page becomes too damaged for anything to be made out. It is possible that they are abbreviation strokes relating to this line (e.g. "na" could be "nam"). However, this doesn't produce any significantly more satisfying readings and letter forms, of which these marks could be the lower portions, can just about be made out above.

3. The slenderisation suggests that this is a prepositional form, hence the tentative translation "{on the} bank".
