Manuscript: Oxford, Bodleian Library, Arch. Selden B.24

The sole manuscript of The Kingis Quare and The Quare of Jealous, Oxford, Bodleian Library MS. Arch. Selden B.24 [Oxford, Bodleian Library, MS. Arch. Seld. B.24.] , which was produced sometime during the reign of James IV (1488–1513; Boffey & Edwards 1997 [Boffey (1997), Julia (ed.), A. S. G Edwards (ed.), and B.C. Barker-Benfield (ed.), The Works of Geoffrey Chaucer and The Kingis Quair: A Facsimile of Bodleian Library, Oxford, MS. Arch. Selden. B.24, Cambridge: DS Brewer] : 22) and which is also an important early witness to the works of Geoffrey Chaucer (d. 1400) and John Lydate (c. 1370–1451), contains a fragment of a Gaelic dán díreach quatrain. This has been inscribed by Domhnall Gorm, presumably a MacDonald of Sleat, and may be celebrating his relationship with a member of the Lothian-based Sinclair family, who commissioned and owned the manuscript, by lamenting his parting from the. Its fragmentary condition inhibits confident interpretation, however.

Hands

Domhnall Gorm ?Mac Domhnaill (Hand21)

The hand that produced the quatrain is identifiable as Domhnall Gorm only by the signature. This was a popular name among the MacDonalds of Sleat during the 16th and early 17th centuries so it is presumed to be one of this clan's chiefs. Interest in poetry on the part of the clan's senior members is attested in the next century through their patronage of Cathal Mac Mhuirich (Black 1976–78 [] : 332–339). Domhnall Gorm Mór seems to be the leading candidate due to him being known to have visited Edinburgh on several occasions (MacDonald & MacDonald 1904 [MacDonald (1904), Angus, and Archibald MacDonald, The Clan Donald, vol. III, Inverness: The Northern Counties Publishing Company] : 34–35). In the absence of more detailed evidence, any explanation as to how he came to interact with the Sinclairs and their manuscripts must remain speculative.

Domhnall Gorm's hand is neat, professional, and his command of the orthography, language, and poetics of the learned classes appears on the whole to be sound. The possibility that the quatrain and signature were inscribed for Domhnall Gorm should be borne in mind. Assuming it is Domhnall Gorm's own hand, this is an interesting specimen of aristocratic high-level literacy.

Other Activities

No other works in this hand, or attributed to Domhnall Gorm, are known.

Physical Description of Manuscrpt

Writing Surface

Paper; 27 × 18.42cm (Hunt 1895 [Hunt (1895), Richard William, and Falconer Madan, A Summary Catalogue of Western Manuscripts in the Bodleian Library at Oxford, Oxford: Clarendon Press] : vol. 2:1, 614)

Foliation

Foliation is modern

Condition

Fol. 231v is badly eroded in places, leading to the loss of the first two syllables of lines a and c in the quatrain under discussion, as well as some text immediately above that may have offered context. The facsimile by Boffey et al. (1997) includes photographs taken under UV light, which unfortunately do not offer much improvement. However, the parts of the quatrain that have escaped erosion are very clear and present no problems.

Layout of Page

The quatrain is one of a number of notes, including several Scots verses, written in a single broad column in the centre of fol. 231v. The quatrain itself is set out one couplet to a manuscript line.

History of the Manuscript

Summary
Origin

A note in the main body of Arch. Seld. B.24 [Oxford, Bodleian Library, MS. Arch. Seld. B.24.] refers to James IV as king, meaning production of the manuscript must have been begun sometime after 1488 (Boffey et al. 1997 [Boffey (1997), Julia (ed.), A. S. G Edwards (ed.), and B.C. Barker-Benfield (ed.), The Works of Geoffrey Chaucer and The Kingis Quair: A Facsimile of Bodleian Library, Oxford, MS. Arch. Selden. B.24, Cambridge: DS Brewer] : 4). The appearance of the date 1592 on fol. 231v, among the spare leaves towards the end of the manuscript, imply that it was complete by this date, although this is uncertain.

Subsequent History

It is thought that it was produced for the Sinclair family in either Edinburgh or Roslin (Boffey et al. 1997 [Boffey (1997), Julia (ed.), A. S. G Edwards (ed.), and B.C. Barker-Benfield (ed.), The Works of Geoffrey Chaucer and The Kingis Quair: A Facsimile of Bodleian Library, Oxford, MS. Arch. Selden. B.24, Cambridge: DS Brewer] : 11).

Acquisition

The manuscript was bequethed to the Bodleian Library as part of the collection of John Selden (1584–1654) (Brown 1899 [Brown (1899), J. T. T., 'The Authorship of "The Kingis Quair"', Transactions of the Glasgow Archaeological Society 3:1, 93-166] : 93). How it came to be in his collection is not known but the window between his collecting activities and the window in which the manuscript was composed is sufficiently narrow for there not to have been a significant intervening history.

Gaelic Verse Fragment


231v: Domhnall Gorm ?Mac Domhnaill (Hand21)

1.
4. -gap- air 1 cioth na coinne . 2
don bhioth air buain a duille
5. -gap- {{b}ruaigh /alt{b}ruaich }3 laim re linne .
fuair sinne air clar na cruinne


6. misi domnall gorm .

Notes

1. The visibility of text drops to zero to the immediate left of the "a-" of "air", means that this might be the ending of a longer word (three syllables maximum) rather than the preposition air. In fact, lines ab, as now visible, are missing one of the two required perfect internal rhymes, and a word ending in "-uair" (: "buain") would supply such a rhyme. Nothing more can be said about this hypothesised word, unfortunately.

2. Some pen strokes are visible just above this line, before the page becomes too damaged for anything to be made out. It is possible that they are abbreviation strokes relating to this line (e.g. "na" could be "nam"). However, this doesn't produce any significantly more satisfying readings and letter forms, of which these marks could be the lower portions, can just about be made out above.

3. The slenderisation suggests that this is a prepositional form, hence the tentative translation "{on the} bank".
