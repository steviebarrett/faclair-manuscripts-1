Notes

1. The first 6 words of the text are now completely lost but seem to have been well-preserved when Black examined them in the 1970s.
2. As Black points out, this could either be the noun frecrae or the verb fris-gair. If the latter, it is an example of a lenited Scottish vernacular infinitive without do.
1. This could be an otherwise unattested adjective based on áthaba (e.g. "athabhuidh{iocht}"), meaning "helebore-like".
2. If the syntax here is meant to parallel previous lines, the scribe may understand "connuirc" to be the verbal noun.
1. This could mean the shore of (the isle of) Coll or simply a description ("the shore of hazels"). If "Tráigh Coll" is a some other placename elsewhere, I have not been able to identify it.
2. The scribe has inserted an "e" into "fallach" to make "feallach". The meaning of the latter is unclear and the former makes for a better rhyme (: raghnallach). However, the amended line is saying that Mag Uidhir is not "fallach" ("negligient"), when it is meant to be criticising him. The scribe may thus have been seeking a more positive adjective like "fíalach" ("modest"), for example.
3. Note the preserved neuter noun, "cís".
4. Black, in his HDSG slips (Slip ID 0002809), reads "raghriuigh" but cannot identify or translate this. My tentative intepretation would translate "without the speech of frost" (i.e. clear speech).
1. The edge of the page runs right along the top of the letters in line 1, meaning some accents or abbreviation markers may have been lost. The most obviously required accents have been supplied. Only visible abbreviations are indicated.
2. This adjective is evidently related to fíad ("game") or fíad ("wild land") but the only available lemma for "fiadhach" means "hunting", which would not fit here syntactically unless one imagine a genitive plural along the clunky lines of "of the huntings" or suchlike.
3. This could be taken as the comparative of solus ("brighter the face of Ó Colla") or the noun, soillse ("sunshine"), in an implied copula construction ("The face of Ó Colla [is] sunshine"). Ther problem with the former is that "dech", in line b, already supplies a comparative.
1. This date is in a different ink (black) from the rest of the text and is also seriously eroded, possibly from an attempt to rub it out.
2. This looks like Niall MacMhuirich's hand, using a different pen and ink (black instead of brown).
1. Confusingly, "dia" could mean either "for his [brother]" or "from his [brother]". While one might expect Partholon to be attempting to disrupt his brother's succession in his own favour, the fact that the passage goes on to state that his brother assumed the kingship with no apparent conflict with Parthalon suggests that Parthalon was acting on his brother's behalf.
