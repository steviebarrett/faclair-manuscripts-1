Manuscript: London British Library MS. Egerton 2899

London, British Library, MS Egerton 2899, consists of a 14th-century psalter and a 15th-century (?) martyrology owned by the Campbells of Glenorchy through the 16th to the 18th century. In around 1500, 'Aingil De dom dhín', a Middle Gaelic verse lorica invoking the seven archangels and the twelve apostles, was inscribed on the flyleaf. On the whole, beyond names of angels and apostles, it consists of praise of their virtues and allusions to the dangers against which they protect the poet. In terms of language, there are some interesting early specimens and also some distinctive scribal traits that merit further investigation as possible examples of Scottish dialectal influence.

Hands

A single hand (FnaG: Hand7) produces the entire text of 'Aingil De dom dhín'. This hand does not appear anywhere else in Egerton 2899 and the manuscript contains no explicit clues as to the scribe's identity. Its cataloguers (BL n.d.: sub num. Egerton MS 2899; Flower 1926: 2) date the hand palaeographically to around 1500.

Black (2011) has speculated that he may have been a MacEwen, for two reasons:

  • The manuscript is associated with the Campbells of Glenorchy (see below), to whom the MacEwens traditionally provided scholars.

  • Certain unusual palaeographical features of this hand (see below) are shared with Neil MacEwen (fl. 1630).

The same scribe is also the sole hand in the first fragment (fols. 1–5) bound into the composite manuscript, Edinburgh, NLS Adv., MS 72.1.31. This fragment preserves at least 13 poems in his hand about or attributed to Colum Cille, although its poor condition frustrates close examination of many of them. The genre as whole has been examined in detail by Herbert (2005). Four of these poems, as well as a text of 'Aingil De dom dhín', appear in Oxford, Bodleiain Library, MS. Laud Misc 615, which Herbert and O'Sullivan (1973: 175) date to 1532×1545 and associate with the circle of scholars working on Columban material for the Ó Néills of Tyrone, NW Ireland. Furthermore, the page layout of the first fragment in NLS Adv. 72.1.31 — oblong pages with long lines, often one quatrain per line — resembles that of Laud Misc. 615. Indeed, the quatrains of 'Aingil De dom dhín' in Egerton 2899 are each also set out one to a line. This might suggest that the present scribe had intellectual contact with scholars in the north of Ireland and drew on similar literary resources.

Contents

'Aingil De dom dhín' is the only Gaelic text in the manuscript; the martyrology shows a particular interest in Argyll saints but it is entirely in Latin, as is the psalter.

Filiation of Text

Thomas O'Nolan (1912) has edited 'Aingil De dom dhín'. He did not use Egerton 2899. Instead, he used eight Irish manuscripts:

  • Dublin, Royal Irish Academy, MS 23.P.16 (1230), 15th cent., An Leabhar Breac

  • Oxford, Bodleian Library, MS Laud Misc. 615, 16th century

  • Dublin, Royal Irish Academy, MS 23.G.23 (256), 17th century

  • Dublin, Royal Irish Academy, MS 23.N.13 (???), date unknown

  • Maynooth, St Patrick’s College, Murphy MS. 70, date unknown

  • Maynooth, St Patrick’s College, Murphy MS. 39, date unknown

  • Maynooth, St Patrick’s College, O’Renehan MS. 96, date unknown

  • A copy in the O’Curry Mss. (unspecified), 19th century

The number and order of the quatrains in the different manuscript versions varies quite significantly. This can be used to show Egerton 2899’s affiliation in the tradition.

The Egerton 2899 text thus seems to align structurally with the Leabhar Breac and Laud Misc. 615 texts. This is not particularly borne out by a comparison between the varia lectiones in O'Nolan's edition and the present witness, however. While the Egerton 2899 text never closely aligns in any serious way with the others against the Leabhar Breac and Laud Misc. 615, it often presents its own distinctive readings against them.

It seems fairly likely that the omission of O'Nolan's q7 was a mistake at a certain point in the tradition. O'Nolan's qq5–7 list seven archangels, describing each of them as a “guarantor” (cuir). O'Nolan's q8 (= the present text's q7) then calls them “the seven guarantors on God's behalf” ('Seacht cuir dar cend De'). Given that three of these guarantors (Raphael, Painiel, and Gabriel) are enumerated in O'Nolan's q7, it was almost certainly in the original composition. The Leabhar Breac, Laud Misc. 615, and Egerton 2899 texts thus all have a common ancestor somewhere below the archetype where this omission occured. This archetype must have existed by the period 1408×1411, when the Leabhar Breac was produced.

From the evidence of textual history, attestation in the manuscript record and language (see below), we can conclude that the poem was originally composed in Ireland, probably in the Middle Gaelic period.


In the linguistic survey that follows, I focus on the Egerton 2899 text of 'Aingil Dé dom dhin'.

The text may have been written originally in early Middle Gaelic, as it preserves some forms and is largely compliant with the norms of Middle Gaelic. However, there are several Early Modern forms that possibly appeared during the poem's transmission or possibly call for the dating of its composition later in the Middle Gaelic period rather than earlier. While the poem was inscribed in Argyll by a Scottish scribe, there seems to be negligible influence from Scottish Gaelic.

It should be noted that the poem does not provide a great deal of linguistic data that could be used to ascertain date or provenance; it includes relatively few verbs, for example, and makes no references to secular history.

Old Gaelic or conservative Middle Gaelic features include:

  • A long a-preterite is retained: do róith (:"cáich") (q12a.

  • Continued use of infixed pronouns: e.g. rom féith ('who watches over me'), q6b, but see also q26b; nachum gabsat gaí ('spears that did not transfix me'). q27b; ním ria ('wil not reach me')q12c.

  • Old Gaelic 1st plural present, atchiam (:biadh, q1b.

  • Old Gaelic 3rd singular future, ním ria ('wil not reach me')q12c, although eDILhas examples of ria from Early Modern Gaelic poetry.

Later Middle and Early Modern Gaelic features include:

  • Object pronoun used with verb other than the copula: fetheat me ('may they watch over me')q26b.

  • Subject pronoun used with verb other than the copula: nach mhillter me tríbh ('that I am not troubled through them'), q3b

  • Elision occurs on 8/10 instances where elision would be possible in Early Modern Gaelic: 1b, 6d, 8d, 12c, 12d, 18c, 20c, 25d, ; the two exceptions are 9c and 29b

Possible signs of Scottish Gaelic influence :

  • One instance of the object of fri being in the dative: "re bais bhan", q17d,

Resistance of Scottish Gaelic influence:

  • 'Irish' passive form used; e.g., gebhar leam gach laí ('is seized on by me each day'), q7d.

  • Numerals 7-10 still nasalise: e.g., Seacht nárćhaingíl ('seven archangels'), q2a.

  • 'Irish' eclipsis is used: parrthalon na b[p]eann ('Parrthalón of the pains'), q20b

It should be noted that these are merely examples of the different influences on the text and are not proportional to one another. The bulk of the text seems to be Middle Gaelic.

History of the Manuscript

Summary

Origin

According to Flower (1926: 23), Egerton 2899 began life in the 14th century as a Psalter.

Subsequent History

About a century later, this was joined by a tabulated martyrology. The latter’s emphasis is on Scottish saints, some very local to Argyll. This, combined with an inscription implying that the manuscript was the property of Colin Campbell, Laird of Glenorchy (d. 1523), and a 18th-century bookplate of his descendants, the Earls of Breadalbane suggests an Argyll provenance for the gathering containing the martyrology. 'Aingil Dé dom dhín' would have been added in this context (around 1500). Technically, the Psalter could be from elsewhere.It has been pointed out (1926: 23) that Colin's father, Duncan, is attributed poetry in the Book of the Dean of Lismore, implying sustained interest in Gaelic literature on the part of this family.

Acquisition

The manuscript was purchased for the British Museum's collections from Sotheby's in February 1912. How it came to be up for sale is unclear.

Encoding of this Transcription

Issues

This transcription raised few particular editorial issues that required deviation from standard encoding practice. On a few occasions, an editorial emendation of highly corrupt forms have been supplied from O'Nolan's edition (1912) using the <corr> element, which is usually used for editorial interventions by personnel on the FnaG project.

Metrics

The poem's metre is rannaigecht bec (51, 51, 51, 51). The text is laid out with one quatrain per line in Egerton 2899.
