Manuscript: London British Library MS. Egerton 2899

London, British Library, MS Egerton 2899, consists of a 14th-century psalter and a 15th-century (?) martyrology owned by the Campbells of Glenorchy through the 16th to the 18th century. In around 1500, 'Aingil De dom dhín', a Middle Gaelic verse lorica invoking the seven archangels and the twelve apostles, was inscribed on the flyleaf. On the whole, beyond names of angels and apostles, it consists of praise of their virtues and allusions to the dangers against which they protect the poet. In terms of language, there are some interesting early specimens and also some distinctive scribal traits that merit further investigation as possible examples of Scottish dialectal influence.

Hands

The Scribe
Identification

A single hand (FnaG: Hand7) produces the entire text of 'Aingil De dom dhín'. This hand does not appear anywhere else in Egerton 2899 and the manuscript contains no explicit clues as to the scribe's identity. Its cataloguers (BL n.d.: sub num. Egerton MS 2899; Flower 1926: 23) dates the hand palaeographically to c.1500.

Black (2011: NLS Adv. MS 72.1.31) has speculated that he may have been a MacEwen, for two reasons:

  • The manuscript was in the possession of the Campbells of Glenorchy by 1523 at latest (see below); the MacEwens traditionally provided scholars to this family.

  • Certain unusual palaeographical features of this hand (see below) are shared with Neil MacEwen (fl. 1630).

Other Activities

The same scribe is also the sole hand in the first fragment (fols. 1–5) bound into the composite manuscript, Edinburgh, NLS Adv., MS 72.1.31. This fragment preserves at least 13 poems in his hand about or attributed to Colum Cille, although its poor condition frustrates close examination of many of them. The genre as whole has been examined in detail by Herbert (2005). Four of these poems, as well as a text of 'Aingil De dom dhín', appear in Oxford, Bodleiain Library, MS. Laud Misc 615, which Herbert and O'Sullivan (1973: 175) date to 1532×1545 and associate with the circle of scholars working on Columban material for the Ó Néills of Tyrone, NW Ireland. Furthermore, the page layout of the first fragment in NLS Adv. 72.1.31 — oblong pages with long lines, often one quatrain per line — resembles that of Laud Misc. 615. Indeed, the quatrains of 'Aingil De dom dhín' in Egerton 2899 are each also set out one to a line. This might suggest that the present scribe had intellectual contact with scholars in the north of Ireland and drew on similar literary resources.

'Aingil De dom dhín' is the only Gaelic text in the manuscript; the martyrology shows a particular interest in Argyll saints (Flower 1926: 23) but it is entirely in Latin, as is the psalter.

Text Summary
Content

The poem is a lorica-style litany, invoking the protection of the seven archangels (one for each day of the week) and the twelve apostles, praising their merits, and alluding to the dangers from which through them the dovotee is preserved. Some manuscripts attribute the poem to Colum Cille (Flower 1926: 24) but these are all late witnesses. Such an ascription is absent from earlier manuscripts, including Egerton 2899, and there is nothing in the text itself to particularly support it. It does, however, have a tendency to be selected by early scribes interested in Columban material (.i.e. the present scribe and the compilers of Laud Misc. 615)

Structure

The poem is bipartite. qq1–18 (in the Egerton 2899 text) invoke the protection of the seven archangels, while qq.19–30 invoke the proetection of the twelve apostles.

O'Nolan (1912: 253) suggests that these might have originally been two separate poems, as the section on the archangels closes with a dúnad. This seems plausible, as neither section depends on the other for sense, and the final quatrain, bearing a unifying dúnad, does not relate specifically to the second section. However, a dúnad need not always indicate the end of a poem but can indicate merely a shift in topic. Also, as shown below, all manuscript witnesses preserve at least some of both sections. Thus, if they were originally separate, their conjunction probably happened prior to the text's archetype and nothing can be known of their independent textual history. Nonetheless, the possibility of distinct origins should be borne in mind when assessing the language.

Style

The metre is lethrannaigecht mór (51515151; abcb). The resulting short lines rarely come together within a quatrain to form a long developed sentence and more often contain a discrete prepositional phrase or short statement. The relentless interest throughout is the glorifying of God, the archangels, and the apostles, so the style remains distinctly panegyrical.

Social Context and Intended Readers

There is no glossing or annotation on 'Aingil De dom dhín' to indicate how readers interacted with the text. As it is placed on the flyleaf of a martyrology/psalter that was, or would shortly come to be, in the possession of an aristocratic family, one might speculate that the poem was used for devotional purposes. Just as the martyrology identifies feast days through the year and the psalter is drawn upon regularly in liturgical time, 'Aingil De dom dhín' identifies a heavenly protector for each day of each week. Whether the Campbells of Glenorchy and their associates would have struggled with the poem's occasional early forms should be considered, however; the poem could be more for ornamental value. On the other hand, that secular 16th-century Scottish elites were capable of appreciating Middle Gaelic texts would be of sociolinguistic interest.

Studies and Editions

The only edition of the text (which does not draw on Egerton 2899) is that of Thomas O'Nolan (1912). Flower (1926: 23–24) comments briefly upon it in his entry on Egerton 2899 in his Catalogue of Irish Manuscripts in the British Museum: Vol. 2. Ronald Black (2016: §128) repeats some of the essential details from O'Nolan and Flower and comments on the hand in the context of its contribution to Edinburgh, NLS, Adv. MS 72.1.31 (2011: NLS Adv. MS 72.1.31). Any further extant work on the text is yet to be identified.

Filiation of Text

When Thomas O'Nolan (1912) edited 'Aingil De dom dhín', he did not use Egerton 2899. Instead, he used eight Irish manuscripts:

  • Dublin, Royal Irish Academy, MS 23.P.16 (1230), 15th cent., An Leabhar Breac

  • Oxford, Bodleian Library, MS Laud Misc. 615, 16th century

  • Dublin, Royal Irish Academy, MS 23.G.23 (256), 17th century

  • Dublin, Royal Irish Academy, MS 23.N.13 (???), date unknown

  • Maynooth, St Patrick’s College, Murphy MS. 70, date unknown

  • Maynooth, St Patrick’s College, Murphy MS. 39, date unknown

  • Maynooth, St Patrick’s College, O’Renehan MS. 96, date unknown

  • A copy in the O’Curry Mss. (unspecified), 19th century

The number and order of the quatrains in the different manuscript versions varies quite significantly. This can be used to show Egerton 2899’s affiliation within the tradition.

The Egerton 2899 text aligns structurally with the Leabhar Breac and Laud Misc. 615 texts. This is not particularly borne out by a comparison between the varia lectiones in O'Nolan's edition and the present witness, however. While the Egerton 2899 text does not consistently align with the others against the Leabhar Breac and Laud Misc. 615, it often presents its own distinctive readings against them.

It seems fairly likely that the omission of O'Nolan's q7 was a mistake at a certain point in the tradition. That is, it is not a later addition. O'Nolan's qq5–7 (represented by qq. 7 (= 5) and 8 (= 7) in Egerton 2899) list seven archangels, describing each of them as a “guarantor” (cuir). O'Nolan's q8 (= q7 in the Egerton 2899 text) then calls them “the seven guarantors on God's behalf” ('Seacht cuir dar cend De'). Given that three of these guarantors (Raphael, Painiel, and Gabriel) are enumerated in O'Nolan's q7, it was almost certainly in the original composition. The Leabhar Breac, Laud Misc. 615, and Egerton 2899 texts thus all have a common ancestor somewhere below the archetype where this omission occured. This archetype must have existed by the period 1408×1411, when the Leabhar Breac was produced.

As all other manuscripts of 'Aingil De dom dhín' are of Irish provenance, it seems reasonable to conclude in the absence of any other evidence that the poem was composed in Ireland. A relatively early stratum in the language suggests it was composed in the Middle Gaelic period.


Language
Spelling System

  • Vowels
  • A mixture of "ea" and "e" is used (e.g. "deamhan" (q29c), "spregadh" (q29d)), with "-ea-" predominating.

  • There are no instances of "io" for "i".

  • There is occasional heterodox treatment of dipthongs (e.g. "naeime" (q12d), acc. (or dat.) of noíbe). Sometimes, they are even simplified to a single vowel ("badal" (q15d), nom. sing. of báegul). Otherwise, the expected Middle Gaelic form of the dipthong is typically used (e.g. "naem" (q19c), nom. sing. of noíb; "taebh" (q28b), dat. sing. of taeb).

  • Unstressed final syllables occasionally show signs of the confusion and simplification of the vowel to /ə/ that characterised Middle Gaelic (e.g. "mo [sh]laintisi air" (q9c), nom. sing. of sláinte). However, they tend to remain appropriate to the grammatical case and number (e.g. "gom cridhe" (q28c), acc. sing. of críde.

  • A distinctive feature of this hand is the use of "y" for "i" (Black (2011: NLS Adv. MS 72.1.31), particularly in non-Gaelic names. This occurs in the present text: "symon" (q20d).

  • Accents
  • Accents are used, but erratically (e.g. "Da apstal dég de" (q19a, recte "Dá apstal dég dé").

  • There is a specific editorial problem with "i". The scribe routinely differentiates "i" from other minims (even when "i" is not in a minim cluster) through the use of an upward stroke that is inditinguishable from his accent. It is thus not possible to tell when, if ever, the scribe intended to mark a long "i" as í.

  • Accents are also quite erratically placed, sometimes even appearing over neighbouring consonants.

  • Consonants
  • Voiced and unvoiced dental stops in final position are usually lenited (e.g. "gruadh" (q14a); "bith" (q15d)), but sometimes not (e.g. "urcanead" (q18a)).

  • Unaspirated voiceless velar stops in final position are typically not lenited (e.g. "sluag" (q1d)), with only one exception ("dluigh" (q4c)).

  • Aspirated voiceless velar stops (/k/) are frequently used in place of aspirated voiceless velar fricatives (/x/) at word end (e.g. "toseac[h]" (q25b); "nac[h]" (q10d)). While not universal, this is occurs too regularly to be explicable by accidental omission of lenition marks.

  • Lenition of bilabial stop "b" in final position to a labio-dental fricative is typically shown (e.g. "cliabh" (q28c)).

  • Lenition of bilabial nasal "m" in final position to a labio-dental fricative is sometimes shown (e.g. "ad chiamh" (q18d)) but more often not (e.g. "niamh" (q6d)).

  • Final resonant "-nn" (/N/) is sometimes rendered as Middle Gaelic hypercorrection "-nd". For example, "ceand" is used throughout for cenn (e.g. "ceand" (q7a)); "-nd" is generally preferred in the text overall by 6:1.

  • A dot, or sometimes a very small tick, is occasionally placed over the "d" in final resonant "-nd", giving "-nḋ" (e.g. "sginḋ" (q30a)); this appears to be indicating that it is part of a consonant cluster and should not be taken as lenition, even though it resembles a lenition mark visually.

  • Initial Mutations
  • Lenition is marked (via a small tick) quite sproradically (e.g. "mo chul" (q22b), "dom dhin" (q29c), "a bharr" (q5d), "mo [sh]laintisi" (q9c); but "mo comla" (q25c), "os ma cind" (q25d)).

  • On two occasions, lenition is marked where nasalisation would be expected ("na cheall" (q2c), "of the churches"; "na fheart" (q10a), "of the virtues").

In the linguistic survey that follows, I focus on the Egerton 2899 text of 'Aingil Dé dom dhin'.

The text may have been written originally in early Middle Gaelic, as it preserves some forms and is largely compliant with the norms of Middle Gaelic. However, there are several Early Modern forms that possibly appeared during the poem's transmission or possibly call for the dating of its composition later in the Middle Gaelic period rather than earlier. While the poem was inscribed in Argyll by a Scottish scribe, there seems to be negligible influence from Scottish Gaelic.

It should be noted that the poem does not provide a great deal of linguistic data that could be used to ascertain date or provenance; it includes relatively few verbs, for example, and makes no references to secular history.

Old Gaelic or conservative Middle Gaelic features include:

  • A long a-preterite is retained: do róith (:"cáich") (q12a.

  • Continued use of infixed pronouns: e.g. rom féith ('who watches over me'), q6b, but see also q26b; nachum gabsat gaí ('spears that did not transfix me'). q27b; ním ria ('wil not reach me')q12c.

  • Old Gaelic 1st plural present, atchiam (:biadh, q1b.

  • Old Gaelic 3rd singular future, ním ria ('wil not reach me')q12c, although eDILhas examples of ria from Early Modern Gaelic poetry.

Later Middle and Early Modern Gaelic features include:

  • Object pronoun used with verb other than the copula: fetheat me ('may they watch over me')q26b.

  • Subject pronoun used with verb other than the copula: nach mhillter me tríbh ('that I am not troubled through them'), q3b

  • Elision occurs on 8/10 instances where elision would be possible in Early Modern Gaelic: 1b, 6d, 8d, 12c, 12d, 18c, 20c, 25d, ; the two exceptions are 9c and 29b

Possible signs of Scottish Gaelic influence :

  • One instance of the object of fri being in the dative: "re bais bhan", q17d,

Resistance of Scottish Gaelic influence:

  • 'Irish' passive form used; e.g., gebhar leam gach laí ('is seized on by me each day'), q7d.

  • Numerals 7-10 still nasalise: e.g., Seacht nárćhaingíl ('seven archangels'), q2a.

  • 'Irish' eclipsis is used: parrthalon na b[p]eann ('Parrthalón of the pains'), q20b

It should be noted that these are merely examples of the different influences on the text and are not proportional to one another. The bulk of the text seems to be Middle Gaelic.

History of the Manuscript

Summary

Origin

According to Flower (1926: 23), Egerton 2899 began life in the 14th century as a Psalter.

Subsequent History

About a century later, this was joined by a tabulated martyrology. The latter’s emphasis is on Scottish saints, some very local to Argyll. This, combined with an inscription implying that the manuscript was the property of Colin Campbell, Laird of Glenorchy (d. 1523), and a 18th-century bookplate of his descendants, the Earls of Breadalbane suggests an Argyll provenance for the gathering containing the martyrology. 'Aingil Dé dom dhín' would have been added in this context (around 1500). Technically, the Psalter could be from elsewhere.It has been pointed out (1926: 23) that Colin's father, Duncan, is attributed poetry in the Book of the Dean of Lismore (Edinburgh, NLS, Adv. MS 72.1.37), implying sustained interest in Gaelic literature on the part of this family.

Acquisition

The manuscript was purchased for the British Museum's collections from Sotheby's in February 1912. How it came to be up for sale is unclear.

Encoding of this Transcription

Issues

This transcription raised few particular editorial issues that required deviation from standard encoding practice. On a few occasions, an editorial emendation of highly corrupt forms have been supplied from O'Nolan's edition (1912) using the <corr> element, which is usually used for editorial interventions by personnel on the FnaG project.The scribe regularly identifies the letter "i" with an un upward stroke above the character resembling a length mark. Unfortunately, it is indistinguishable visually from the length marks he adds to other characters. It is thus not possible to tell when, if ever, he intended to add a length mark to an "i". For this reason, all length marks on "i" have been removed in the transcription.

Metrics

The poem's metre is leth-rannaigecht bec (51, 51, 51, 51). The text is laid out with one quatrain per line in Egerton 2899.
