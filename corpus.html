Manuscript: London British Library MS. Egerton 2899

London, British Library, MS Egerton 2899REF TEXT HERE, consists of a 14th-century psalter and a 15th-century (?) martyrology owned by the Campbells of Glenorchy through the 16th to the 18th century. In around 1500, 'Aingil Dé dom dhínREF TEXT HERE', a Middle Gaelic verse lorica invoking the seven archangels and the twelve apostles, was inscribed on the flyleaf. On the whole, beyond names of angels and apostles, it consists of praise of their virtues and allusions to the dangers against which they protect the poet. In terms of language, there are some interesting early specimens and also some distinctive scribal traits that merit further investigation as possible examples of Scottish dialectal influence.

Hands

The Scribe
Identification

A single hand (FnaG: Hand7REF TEXT HERE) produces the entire text of 'Aingil De dom dhínREF TEXT HERE'. This hand does not appear anywhere else in Egerton 2899REF TEXT HERE and the manuscript contains no explicit clues as to the scribe's identity. Its cataloguers (BL n.d.REF TEXT HERE: sub num. Egerton MS 2899; Flower 1926REF TEXT HERE: 23) dates the hand palaeographically to c.1500.

Black (2011REF TEXT HERE: NLS Adv. MS 72.1.31) has speculated that he may have been a MacEwen, for two reasons:

  • The manuscript was in the possession of the Campbells of Glenorchy by 1523 at latest (see below); the MacEwens traditionally provided scholars to this family.

  • Certain unusual palaeographical features of this hand (see below) are shared with Neil MacEwen (fl. 1630).

Other Activities

The same scribe is also the sole hand in the first fragment (fols. 1–5) bound into the composite manuscript, Edinburgh, NLS Adv., MS 72.1.31REF TEXT HERE. This fragment preserves at least 13 poems in his hand about or attributed to Colum Cille, although its poor condition frustrates close examination of many of them. The genre as whole has been examined in detail by Herbert (2005REF TEXT HERE). Four of these poems, as well as a text of 'Aingil De dom dhín', appear in Oxford, Bodleiain Library, MS. Laud Misc 615, which Herbert and O'Sullivan (1973REF TEXT HERE: 175) date to 1532×1545 and associate with the circle of scholars working on Columban material for the Ó Néills of Tyrone, NW Ireland. Furthermore, the page layout of the first fragment in NLS Adv. 72.1.31REF TEXT HERE — oblong pages with long lines, often one quatrain per line — resembles that of Laud Misc. 615. Indeed, the quatrains of 'Aingil De dom dhín' in Egerton 2899REF TEXT HERE are each also set out one to a line. This might suggest that the present scribe had intellectual contact with scholars in the north of Ireland and drew on similar literary resources.

Text Summary
Content

The poem is a lorica-style litany, invoking the protection of the seven archangels (one for each day of the week) and the twelve apostles, praising their merits, and alluding to the dangers from which through them the dovotee is preserved. Some manuscripts attribute the poem to Colum Cille (Flower 1926REF TEXT HERE: 24) but these are all late witnesses. Such an ascription is absent from earlier manuscripts, including Egerton 2899REF TEXT HERE, and there is nothing in the text itself to particularly support it. It does, however, have a tendency to be selected by early scribes interested in Columban material (.i.e. the present scribe and the compilers of Laud Misc. 615REF TEXT HERE)

Structure

The poem is bipartite. qq1–18 (in the Egerton 2899REF TEXT HERE text) invoke the protection of the seven archangels, while qq.19–30 invoke the proetection of the twelve apostles.

O'Nolan (1912REF TEXT HERE: 253) suggests that these might have originally been two separate poems, as the section on the archangels closes with a dúnad. This seems plausible, as neither section depends on the other for sense, and the final quatrain, bearing a unifying dúnad, does not relate specifically to the second section. However, a dúnad need not always indicate the end of a poem but can indicate merely a shift in topic. Also, as shown below, all manuscript witnesses preserve at least some of both sections. Thus, if they were originally separate, their conjunction probably happened prior to the text's archetype and nothing can be known of their independent textual history. Nonetheless, the possibility of distinct origins should be borne in mind when assessing the language.

Style

The metre is lethrannaigecht mór (51515151; abcb). The resulting short lines rarely come together within a quatrain to form a long developed sentence and more often contain a discrete prepositional phrase or short statement. The relentless interest throughout is the glorifying of God, the archangels, and the apostles, so the style remains distinctly panegyrical.

Social Context and Intended Readers

There is no glossing or annotation on 'Aingil Dé dom dhínREF TEXT HERE' to indicate how readers interacted with the text. As it is placed on the flyleaf of a martyrology/psalter that was, or would shortly come to be, in the possession of an aristocratic family, one might speculate that the poem was used for devotional purposes. Just as the martyrology identifies feast days through the year and the psalter is drawn upon regularly in liturgical time, 'Aingil Dé dom dhínREF TEXT HERE' identifies a heavenly protector for each day of each week. Whether the Campbells of Glenorchy and their associates would have struggled with the poem's occasional early forms should be considered, however: the poem could be more for ornamental value. On the other hand, the poem reseambles secular panegyric in terms of metre, imagery and tone. It has been pointed out (Flower 1926REF TEXT HERE: 23) that Colin's father, Duncan, is attributed poetry in the Book of the Dean of Lismore (Edinburgh, NLS, Adv. MS 72.1.37REF TEXT HERE), implying sustained interest in Gaelic literature on the part of this family.

The Egerton 2899REF TEXT HERE text aligns structurally with the Leabhar BreacREF TEXT HERE and Laud Misc. 615REF TEXT HERE texts. This is not particularly borne out by a comparison between the varia lectiones in O'Nolan's edition and the present witness, however. While the Egerton 2899REF TEXT HERE text does not consistently align with the others against the Leabhar BreacREF TEXT HERE and Laud Misc. 615REF TEXT HERE, it often presents its own distinctive readings against them.

It seems fairly likely that the omission of O'Nolan's q7 was a mistake at a certain point in the tradition. That is, it is not a later addition. O'Nolan's qq5–7 (represented by qq. 7 (= 5) and 8 (= 7) in Egerton 2899REF TEXT HERE) list seven archangels, describing each of them as a “guarantor” (cuir). O'Nolan's q8 (= q7 in the Egerton 2899REF TEXT HERE text) then calls them “the seven guarantors on God's behalf” ('Seacht cuir dar cend De'). Given that three of these guarantors (Raphael, Painiel, and Gabriel) are enumerated in O'Nolan's q7, it was almost certainly in the original composition. The Leabhar BreacREF TEXT HERE, Laud Misc. 615REF TEXT HERE, and Egerton 2899REF TEXT HERE texts thus all have a common ancestor somewhere below the archetype where this omission occured. This archetype must have existed by the period 1408×1411, when the Leabhar BreacREF TEXT HERE was produced.

As all other manuscripts of 'Aingil Dé dom dhínREF TEXT HERE' are of Irish provenance, it seems reasonable to conclude in the absence of any other evidence that the poem was composed in Ireland. A relatively early stratum in the language suggests it was composed in the Middle Gaelic period.


Language and Style

The survey that follows draws solely on the Egerton 2899REF TEXT HERE text unless otherwise specified, as the present concern is with the language of this scribe and with this version of the poem as a Scottish literary artefact.

Orthography and Scribal Practices
Spelling System

  • Vowels
  • A mixture of "ea" and "e" is used (e.g. "deamhan" (q29cREF TEXT HERE), "spregadh" (q29dREF TEXT HERE)), with "-ea-" predominating.

  • There are no instances of "io" for "i".

  • There is occasional heterodox treatment of dipthongs (e.g. "naeime" (q12dREF TEXT HERE), acc. (or dat.) of noíbe). Sometimes, they are even simplified to a single vowel ("badal" (q15dREF TEXT HERE), nom. sing. of báegul). Otherwise, the expected Middle Gaelic form of the dipthong is typically used (e.g. "naem" (q19cREF TEXT HERE), nom. sing. of noíb; "taebh" (q28bREF TEXT HERE), dat. sing. of taeb).

  • Unstressed final syllables occasionally show signs of the confusion and simplification of the vowel to /ə/ that characterised Middle Gaelic (e.g. "mo [sh]laintisi air" (q9cREF TEXT HERE), nom. sing. of sláinte). However, they tend to remain appropriate to the grammatical case and number (e.g. "gom cridhe" (q28cREF TEXT HERE), acc. sing. of críde.

  • A distinctive feature of this hand is the use of "y" for "i" (Black (2011REF TEXT HERE: NLS Adv. MS 72.1.31), particularly in non-Gaelic names. This occurs in the present text: "symon" (q20dREF TEXT HERE).

  • On two occasions, ríg is used for nominative (or possibly vocative) rí (q28dREF TEXT HERE, q29aREF TEXT HERE), anticipating Scottish Gaelic rìogh.

  • Accents
  • Accents are used, but erratically (e.g. "Da apstal dég de" (q19aREF TEXT HERE, recte "Dá apstal dég dé").

  • There is a specific editorial problem with "i". The scribe routinely differentiates "i" from other minims (even when "i" is not in a minim cluster) through the use of an upward stroke that is inditinguishable from his accent. It is thus not possible to tell when, if ever, the scribe intended to mark a long "i" as í.

  • Accents are also quite erratically placed, sometimes even appearing over neighbouring consonants.

  • Consonants
  • Voiced and unvoiced dental stops in final position are usually lenited (e.g. "gruadh" (q14aREF TEXT HERE); "bith" (q15dREF TEXT HERE)), but sometimes not (e.g. "urcanead" (q18aREF TEXT HERE)).

  • Unaspirated voiceless velar stops in final position are typically not lenited (e.g. "sluag" (q1dREF TEXT HERE)), with only one exception ("dluigh" (q4cREF TEXT HERE)).

  • Aspirated voiceless velar stops (/k/) are frequently used in place of aspirated voiceless velar fricatives (/x/) at word end (e.g. "nac[h]" (q10dREF TEXT HERE); "gac[h] domnaic[h]" (q14cREF TEXT HERE); "toseac[h]" (q25bREF TEXT HERE)). While not universal, this is occurs too regularly to be explicable by accidental omission of lenition marks.

  • Lenition of bilabial stop "b" in final position to a labio-dental fricative is typically shown (e.g. "cliabh" (q28cREF TEXT HERE)).

  • Lenition of bilabial nasal "m" in final position to a labio-dental fricative is sometimes shown (e.g. "ad chiamh" (q18dREF TEXT HERE)) but more often not (e.g. "niamh" (q6dREF TEXT HERE)).

  • Final resonant "-nn" (/N/) is sometimes rendered as Middle Gaelic hypercorrection "-nd". For example, "ceand" is used throughout for cenn (e.g. "ceand" (q7aREF TEXT HERE)); "-nd" is generally preferred in the text overall by 6:1.

  • A dot, or sometimes a very small tick, is occasionally placed over the "d" in final resonant "-nd", giving "-nḋ" (e.g. "sginḋ" (q30aREF TEXT HERE)); this appears to be indicating that it is part of a consonant cluster and should not be taken as lenition, even though it resembles a lenition mark visually.

  • Initial Mutations
  • Lenition is marked (via a small tick) quite sproradically (e.g. "mo chul" (q22bREF TEXT HERE), "dom dhin" (q29cREF TEXT HERE), "a bharr" (q5dREF TEXT HERE), "mo [sh]laintisi" (q9cREF TEXT HERE); but "mo comla" (q25cREF TEXT HERE), "os ma cind" (q25dREF TEXT HERE)).

  • Eclipsis is marked by nasalising initial vowels (e.g. "Seacht nárćhaingil" (q2aREF TEXT HERE)) and voiced stops (e.g. "ar urcanead ngur" (q18aREF TEXT HERE)) and via voicing voiceless stops (e.g. "parrthalon na b[p]eann" (q20bREF TEXT HERE)).

  • On two occasions, lenition is marked where eclipsis would be expected ("na cheall" (q2cREF TEXT HERE), "of the churches"; "na fheart" (q10aREF TEXT HERE), "of the virtues"). This may be intended as a catch-all marker of whatever the appropriate mutation might be.

  • Occasionally, eclipsis is not marked at all (e.g. "toseac[h] na fear find" (q25bREF TEXT HERE)).

  • There are several examples of pronunciation spellings, in which the initial mutation is simplified or disappears entirely (e.g. "na b[p]eann" (q20bREF TEXT HERE); "muire an [fh]irog fhind" (q20cREF TEXT HERE); "ni gloir [fh]and" (q5bREF TEXT HERE).

Scribal Practices

  • Letter Forms
  • Black (2011REF TEXT HERE: NLS Adv. MS 72.1.31) has commented that this scribe employs a number of distinctive forms, compared to the norms of Gaelic scribal pratice at the time. Some of these are instanced in this text: for example, capital "T" consisting an upward stroke with a rightward half-stroke at top and bottom (resembling "[") (e.g. "T́igid" (q24aREF TEXT HERE)) or "y" for "i" in names (e.g. "symon" (q20dREF TEXT HERE)).

  • Double insular "s" is used to stand for "S" at the beginning of quatrains (e.g. "Seacht" (q2aREF TEXT HERE)).

  • When "C" begins a new quatrain, it is slightly enlarged and a dot is placed inside the crescent (e.g. "Cor" (q2aREF TEXT HERE)).

  • All other letters have their own capital form, used when the letter begins a quatrain.

  • The scribe regularly adds thin upward stroke above "i". This is very useful for distinguishing the letter in a minim cluster, but the stroke is indistinguishable from the length mark used elsewhere. It is thus not clear when, if ever, the scribe means to indicate a long "i".

  • Abbreviations
  • All abbreviations used in the text can be described as mainstream within Gaelic manuscript culture.

  • Most stand for just one or two characters (e.g. n-stroke, m-stroke).

  • Generic suspensions, standing for more lengthy strings of characters, are employed very rarely and only in the context of personal names.

  • Word Division
  • Spacing between words is in general quite irregular.

  • There is a tendency to use spacing to section off set prepositional phrases (e.g. "osmachind" (fol.iiir2REF TEXT HERE)), noun phrases ("molaintisi" (i.e. "mo [sh]lainti-si") (fol.iiir9REF TEXT HERE)), and verb complexes (e.g. "nacbi" (fol.iiir17REF TEXT HERE)) and suchlike.

  • The scribe is also capable of rendering a sizeable portion of a quatrain with no notieable spacing between words, phrases, or even metrical lines (e.g. the rest of fol.iiir2REF TEXT HERE; fol.iiir23REF TEXT HERE).

  • Verse Layout
  • Each quatrain is set out in one continuous manuscript line, with only very occasional spillover (e.g. fol.iiir2REF TEXT HERE).

  • A capital letter (indicated various ways) is used to introduce a new quatrain.

  • A punctus is used occasionally to separate metrical lines or quatrains, although it sometimes seems to be misplaced (e.g. q14aREF TEXT HERE).

  • Dúinte are used to indicate the end of the poem (fol.iiir31REF TEXT HERE) and the switchover from angels to apostles (fol.iiir18REF TEXT HERE) (or the joining of two originally independant poems). They occur within the main text and are not sectioned off in any special way.

  • Corrections
  • There are no signs of any corrections having been made to the text by the original scribe or subsequently.

  • Other traits
  • As noted, the scribe not infrequently uses pronunciation spellings (e.g. "na b[p]eann" (q20bREF TEXT HERE)). On other occasions, however, the forms that he renders imply a misunderstanding of the text's sense. For example, he appears to be referring to an eighth (otherwise unknown) archangel when he writes "scaphel blaith | a bharr" (fol.iiir6REF TEXT HERE–7REF TEXT HERE; "Scaphael, fine his helmet"); other manuscript witnesses have "scathfhial" ("sheltering veil") at this point. In another example, he suspiciously repeats "uriel" (fol.iiir10REF TEXT HERE) in the verse on Archangel Uriel at a point where an adjective would be expected (= "ur[fh]iel", air- + fíal?), although there is confusion regarding this line throughout the manuscript witnesses (O'Nolan 1912REF TEXT HERE: 255).

Language
Nominal System

  • Nouns
  • There are several examples of preserved accusatives in the text ("ar urcanead ngur" (q18aREF TEXT HERE); "re gach nimclog nar" (q24bREF TEXT HERE)), although only following prepositions.

  • However, there are also possible examples of the accusative being replaced by the dative (e.g ."re báis ḃha[i]n" (q17dREF TEXT HERE); "fam cliu" (q27aREF TEXT HERE)), although the scribe elsewhere use a enition mark where eclipsis would be expected (see above)).)

  • There is also a potential example of hypercorrect eclipsis being used after the "subject" of a verbal noun as if it were the object of a verb.: "re dul an treas nuilc" (q21bREF TEXT HERE)).

  • Adjectives
  • Adjectives mostly behave as expected. However, there are few examples of adjectives outside the nominative singular, or in the comparitive or superlative, so few diagnostic observations can be made.

  • There are a few examples of adjectives failing to be declined with the dative noun they describe; all concern clé (e.g. "fan chaingin chlé" (q1bREF TEXT HERE); cf. q27aREF TEXT HERE, where the dative form of the substantive (clíu) is used.).

  • Definite Article
  • All plural articles are monosyllabic (i.e. na).

  • There are no examples of articles with dative plural nouns (most happen to be genitive plural), so declension of the article cannot be tested.

  • In one case, a masculine article is used with an originally neuter noun: "an tolc" (q10dREF TEXT HERE).

Verbal System

  • Personal Endings
  • There are no analytic verb forms in the text.

  • A relatively wide range of tenses, numbers, and moods are represented by synthetic forms.

  • Some Old Gaelic person endings are preserved and secured by rhyme (e.g. "at chiam" (:"biadh"; q1bREF TEXT HERE)).

  • There are some examples of Middle Gaelic simple verbs formed from the prototonic form of compound verbs (e.g. "T́igid", from do-icc (q24aREF TEXT HERE)).

  • On one occasion, the relative ending -as is used, although possibly in its original Old Gaelic usage as a relative future "dicras deamhan dim" (q30bREF TEXT HERE; "[breastplate] ... that will drive back (?) a demon from me").

  • Another active relative form resists the Middle Gaelic generalisation of -as: "nac[h] blaisde" (q1dREF TEXT HERE).

  • However, a relative passive plural ("nac[h] mhillter me tribh" (q3bREF TEXT HERE; "so that they do not destroy me (?) through them")) undegoes initial lenition, perhaps showing the Middle Gaelic spread of the lenited relative clause (NB: the passive form with apparently active meaning is also problematic, given that millid is not deponent).

  • Occasionally, consonant confusion distorts the personal ending. For example, "mar tisat eac[h] } ur" (q24cREF TEXT HERE, where the verb is almost certainly singular, despite the "-at" ending (leg. "-ad"(?).)

  • The Verbal Complex
  • There are relatively few preterites/perfects in this text; being oriented to devotion, its focus is on the present and the future.

  • The most common pre-verbal particle used is nach, with the conjunct form of the verb.

  • On its sole occurrence,ro is used perfectively: "re cech ngád (?) rom feith" (q6bREF TEXT HERE; "against each danger he has protected me").

  • There are no occurences of do at all.

  • Infixed, Object, and Subject Pronouns
  • Infixed pronouns are used on several occasions (e.g. "nim ria a naine olc" (q12cREF TEXT HERE)).

  • However, the independant object pronoun mé is also used on several occasions (e.g. "fetheat me ar rith uilc" (q26bREF TEXT HERE)). All instances are found in metrically compliant lines and are also attested in other manuscript witnesses, so this is unlikely to be the product of late scribal slips. It could be a stylistic choice; in what is essentially a prayer, the poet is placing the emphasis on himself as a recipient of divine aid, hence only 1st-person object pronouns being used this way.

  • There are no subject pronouns used in the text.

  • Copula/Substantive
  • The use and forms of the verbs "to be", which do not appear frequently, seem largely standard for conservative Middle Gaelic.

  • There is one occurence of the copula's originally relative form as being used as a main verb ("as e an seachtmadh slan" (q14bREF TEXT HERE)), evidencing Middle Gaelic vowel confusion.

  • The same example also shows another Middle Gaelic feature: a subject pronoun being used to support the copula. This is attested in other manuscript witnesses, although elision means that it cannot be said whether it is essential to the original text.

Pronouns

  • Personal
  • Very few independent personal pronouns are used in the text.

  • Emphatic pronominals and conjugated prepositions are more common.

  • Conjugated Prepositions
  • A prepositions conjugated with a possessive and followed by a noun or verbal noun constitute a very common, quasi-verbal construction in the text (e.g. "Mata maith dom dhin" (q21aREF TEXT HERE)).

  • There is one instance of preposition tre, which takes the accusative in Old Gaelic, being conjugated with a dative ("tribh" (q3bREF TEXT HERE)).

Syntax

  • Verb Phrases
  • A lot of verb phrases in the text are relative or subordinate. The quatrain as a whole usually has an angel or apostle or group thereof as its subject, with an implied optative or imperative verb "to be"; verb phases then occur within the structure relative to this subject.

  • There are no examples of preposition + verb relative construct.

  • With one aforementioned exception ("as e an seachtmadh slan" (q14bREF TEXT HERE)), all instances of the copula and the substantive have a direct predicate.

  • Noun Phrases
  • As already noted, there are several examples of nouns in the accusative but only following appropriate prepositions. There are no clear examples of the accusative as the direct object.

  • There is also one potential hypercorrect use of the accusative to indicate the object of a verbal noun and several instances of the dative playing the role of the accusative.

  • Most lines in the poem consist of either a name and an epithet (e.g. "crist fein fear gan crith" (q15bREF TEXT HERE)) or two noun phrases related to each other by a preposition, often conjugated with a possessive (e.g. "dom saeradh go daith" (q15cREF TEXT HERE)).

  • Adverbials
  • Adverbials are used sparingly in this text.

  • Adverbs are either free-standing words (e.g. "thuas" (q25aREF TEXT HERE)) or formed out of co + adjective (e.g. "go daingin" (q11bREF TEXT HERE)).

Lexis

  • Specialised/Technical Vocabulary
  • Despite angels being a relatively esoteric subject, there are few terms in this text that could be described as specialised or technical.

  • The vocabulary, along with the imagery and sentiments, are reminiscent of secular panegyric. The language is thus far from plain but would be well-understood in mainstream literary terms.

  • Loanwords
  • There are naturally religious or ecclesiastical terms that were originally lonwords from Latin (or from Greek via Latin (e.g. "biaid" (q23bREF TEXT HERE), from beati; "gloir" (q5bREF TEXT HERE), from gloria; "apstal" (q19aREF TEXT HERE), from apostola). However, most of these would have been well-established in Gaelic by the Middle Gaelic period, let alone by 1500.

Conclusions

'Aingil De dom dhínREF TEXT HERE' evidently has a significant early Middle Gaelic stratum and was probably composed in that era. The scribe renders most of the early features accurately. Most of the incongruities (e.g. pronunciation spellings, a curious approach to marking initial mutations) seem to be his own idiosyncracies. There are no widespread or wholesale modernisations that could be attributed to him or his late medieval scribal predcessors. It can therefore be concluded that this early text has been transmitted with reasonable accuracy, generally speaking.

The poem's presentation and context imply that it was meant to be presentable. Abbreviations are used sparingly, there are no glosses or corrections, and it fits most appositely into the manuscript in terms of content. It thus seems reasonable to assume that it was inscribed for devotional use, or at least inspiration, among the Campbells of Glenorchy alongside the martyrology and the psalter. To what extent a Middle Gaelic literary text would have posed comprehension problems in 16th-century Scotland outside of bardic circles is not clear. However, the formal bardic metre and general panegyric style would surely have offered ways into its appreciation for a reasonably cultured audience.

In terms of influence from Scottish Gaelic, this is certainly not widespread. "Ríg" is used for rí, the dative has expanded its role slightly at the expense of the accusative, and there is an instinct towards leniting relatives regardless of context, but these are isolated features and the examples of the latter two features are for the most part problematic. The scribe's consistent preference for "-c" over "-ch" and his erratic handling of initial mutations (sometimes using lenition marks where nasalisation is required, sometimes resorting to pronunciation spellings) perhaps merit further investigation, as they could turn out to represent points of tension between the language of the scribe and the literary Middle Gaelic of his text.

Style

The poem is part devotional and part panegyric. It invokes the protection of the seven archangels and the twelve apostles, then finally Christ and the Holy Spirity, through expressing confidence in their holiness and efficacy. As such, it has much in common with secular praise poetry, with martial imagery regularly invoked to depict never-quite-specified spiritual dangers and their vanquishment by the entity being invoked. For example, Panael is said to act "fo rinḋ cathairm chuir[r]" (q14dREF TEXT HERE: "against the point of a sharpened battle-weapon") and the two apostles called James are "sgiath fail ar mo sgath" (q22dREF TEXT HERE; "a shield upon my back").

The structure is very straightforward. After a short preface praising the seven archangels (qq.1–7), one archangel is invoked for each day of the week (qq8–14). After a concluding set of stanzas on the archangels' collective efficacy (qq.15–18), the twelve apostles are introduced and invoked (qq.19–26. Many apostles share a stanza and Mary's protection is also briefly sought. The poem then ends with invocation of Christ and the Holy Spirit as the ultimate protection (qq.26–30). Despite this methodical structure, the poem is not particularly didactic. One would not learn very much from it about the angels, the apostles, or even about Christ, in terms of historical or theological information. The focus is instead on the needs and subjective faith of the poet/speaker.

Lethrannaigecht Mór, the metre, is not as strict as some in the bardic canon. It requires four lines of five syllables with a monosyllabic cadence, perfect end-rhyme between lines b and d, imperfect rhyme between a word in c and a word in d, and alliteration in each line (51515151, abcb). On the whole, this metre is adhered to throughout the poem in Egerton 2899REF TEXT HERE. Alliteration is occasionally absent (e.g. q24aREF TEXT HERE), there is one hypersyllabic line (q17aREF TEXT HERE), and end-rhyme is imperfect on one occasion (q18bREF TEXT HERE, cREF TEXT HERE) without remedy via straightforward editorial emendation. It is questionable whether the scribe fully understood the demands of the metre, however, as some of his orthographic variants spoil the attendant rhymes. For example, an extra "r" has to be added to "chuir[r]" (q13dREF TEXT HERE) to place it in the same class as "droing" (q13bREF TEXT HERE).

History of the Manuscript

Summary

Origin

According to Flower (1926REF TEXT HERE: 23), Egerton 2899REF TEXT HERE began life in the 14th century as a psalter.

Subsequent History

About a century later, the psalter was joined by a tabulated martyrology. The latter’s emphasis is on Scottish saints, some very local to Argyll. This, combined with an inscription implying that the manuscript was the property of Colin Campbell, Laird of Glenorchy (d. 1523), and a 18th-century bookplate of his descendants, the Earls of Breadalbane suggests an Argyll provenance for the gathering containing the martyrology. 'Aingil Dé dom dhínREF TEXT HERE' would have been added in this context (around 1500). Technically, the psalter could be from elsewhere.

Acquisition

The manuscript was purchased for the British Museum's collections from Sotheby's in February 1912. How it came to be up for sale is unclear.
